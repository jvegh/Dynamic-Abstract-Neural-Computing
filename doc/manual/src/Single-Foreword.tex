\quotationbox
{"Despite the extraordinary diversity and complexity of neuronal morphology and synaptic connectivity,
\textit{the nervous systems adopts a number of
 basic principles}"~\cite{JohnstonWuNeurophysiology:1995}
}


In this chapter, we derive those basic principles and we prepare their discussion in an abstract level.
It the later chapters we will derive them in an explicite form from the first principles of science.
We show that we must rethink our descriptions
of nature, which are based on disciplinarily organized science,
which were systematically tested only for inanimate science.
\index{Schrödinger, Erwin}
In the spirit of Johnston and Wu \cite{JohnstonWuNeurophysiology:1995},
we introduce the relevant major components of a single abstract neuron that still follows the
{basic principles} when processing neuronal information,
moreover, the abstract principles of how they cooperate, conceptually.
Our procedure is essentially the same that John von Neumann followed
when described the operating principles of computers~\cite{EDVACreport1945}:
\index{von Neumann, John}
"we will base our considerations on a hypothetical element, which functions
essentially like a vacuum tube, without going into details". 
We will base our considerations on a hypothetical element, which functions
essentially like a biological neuron, without going into details, an 'abstract neuron'.


It is hard to discuss a non disciplinary operation divided
into disciplinary parts of texts; expecially if we want to
compare some aspects of operation to their independently
developed technical counterparts.
We prepare the concepts and principles which enable us to
answer E. Schrödinger's question concerning the role of physics.
